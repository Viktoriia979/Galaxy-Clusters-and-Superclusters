Galaxy Clusters and Superclusters

Galaxy clusters are huge groups of galaxies that are held together by gravity. Superclusters are even larger structures made up of many galaxy clusters and smaller groups of galaxies. These are some of the biggest structures in the universe and show how galaxies are connected across vast distances.

Galaxy clusters can contain hundreds or even thousands of galaxies and can stretch across millions of light-years. Superclusters are much larger, spanning hundreds of millions of light-years and containing thousands of galaxies. Although they are incredibly large, most of the space between the galaxies is almost completely empty.

One well-known galaxy cluster is the Virgo Cluster, which contains over 1,000 galaxies, including some near the Milky Way. Our Milky Way is part of the Local Group, which belongs to the Laniakea Supercluster. They are called clusters and superclusters because they are giant collections of galaxies grouped together by gravity. Studying these enormous structures helps astronomers understand how the universe is organised and how it has changed over billions of years.
